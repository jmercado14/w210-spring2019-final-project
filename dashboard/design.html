How did we know what to build?
We asked the experts - the end users.

User Centered Design

Our team believes that the best technology is the kind that truly serves its users. So, as we developed the Lysten system, we kept a tight feedback loop with our potential customers. We interviewed students, professors, and administrators from the UC Berkeley MIDS program to find out what features were most important to them. This market research work heavily influenced the design of Lysten, and we pivoted our vision multiple times to best match the needs of our users. In the end, we believe we've created a system that will greatly benefit everyone involved in the online education environment.

Student Reaction to Lysa

We reached out to 30 online students via survey to understand their current frustrations with course feedback systems, and how they would prefer to provide input.

We learned that the timing of course surveys did not actually meet the students' desires; they were asked to provide feedback at the mid- and end-points of the term, but never actually got to see their feedback incorporated into the class. Low turnout rates for these course feedback surveys made it seem like students did not want to provide feedback; in reality, they were just not incentivized to do so, since they never saw the fruits of their effort. In reality, students were willing to provide feedback more frequently than twice a term, provided that they personally reaped some benefit from it. With Lysa, we emphasized the ability to provide feedback at any point during the term, so that professors can get real-time feedback and incorporate changes quickly. We hope that students are encouraged by this reactivity, and will engage proactively with the Lysa as they see their input taken into consideration.

We also learned that students often have feedback about a specific aspect of a course, and wish to provide comments on just that element. However, current course surveys are not set up for such specific focus; you must fill out the entire survey, covering all topics, before you submit. With Lysa, we decided to engage at the single-topic level, so that students can comment on the most relevant aspects of the course in a given moment. For example, they can provide feedback on assignments the week they are given, without having to also comment on the instruction or async materials. We hope that, given the ability to provide more pointed commentary, students will not feel overwhelmed by long surveys and will be more willing to provide feedback.

Faculty Reaction to Dashboards

We interviewed a diverse group of 10 faculty members (including program directors, student success team members, seasoned professors, and even recent students-turned-professors) to understand how online educators digest and incorporate the feedback they receive from students.

We found that, despite an intimidating volume of text, professors really do want to read every comment from every student. While a high-level summary is helpful for context and pattern finding, the individual comments are very valuable themselves. With that in mind, we created the Student Responses dashboard, where professors can see the full text of every student comment. We simply added filters to make the information more digestable.

We learned that point-in-time ratings and scores are not particularly useful, as they can be subjective. In reality, trends over time are far more useful as faculty try to adjust their courses. The Topic Stream dashboard provides this over-time view, so that faculty can see what topics are discussed more or less frequently over the years, terms, and across various courses.

Professors want to incorporate student feedback and make course adjustments, however it is difficult to do so if the comments are vague or over-dramatic. Many faculty members wished for specific examples to be given with course comments, which make feedback more actionable. We reflected this desire in Lysa's conversation style, requesting examples of both positive and negative aspects of the course.

Beta Testing

We developed the initial Lysten system based on the above market research, then conducted a beta phase to get reactions from real users. Students were invited to engage with Lysa directly, and faculty members were allowed to play around with the dashboard screens. We made further refinements based on their reactions and comments.
